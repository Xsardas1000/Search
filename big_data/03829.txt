Recent studies of heavy-fermion systems with tunable quantum Ô¨Çuctuations have focused on a variety of
zero-temperature phase transitions that involve not only the onset of magnetic order but also the destruction of
Kondo entanglement. Motivated by these developments, we investigate the effect of enhanced quantum Ô¨Çuctu-
ations induced by a transverse magnetic Ô¨Åeld in an Ising-anisotropic Kondo lattice model, solved within an ex-
tended dynamical mean Ô¨Åeld theory using the numerical renormalization group. A line of locally critical points
describes a direct transition from a Kondo-screened paramagnetic heavy-fermion state to a Kondo-destroyed
antiferromagnetic phase. Along the line, the extracted critical exponents remain unchanged. By probing the the
interplay between quantum Ô¨Çuctuations of the local moments, the Kondo effect, and magnetic order, this study
provides signiÔ¨Åcant new insight into the global phase diagram of heavy-fermion systems.

Heavy-fermion materials have provided condensed mat-
ter physics with some of its most challenging yet fascinat-
ing problems.
In the recent past, these materials have also
presented a canonical setting to study non-Fermi liquid be-
havior in the context of quantum criticality [1]. Inspired by
the unusual quantum critical properties of prototypical sys-
tems such as YbRh2Si2 [2‚Äì4], CeCu6‚àíxAux [5], and CeRhIn5
[6, 7], several authors [8, 9] proposed the global phase dia-
gram (GPD) sketched in Fig. 1(a).
In the form relevant to
the present work, the GPD addresses the fundamental role of
quantum Ô¨Çuctuations and their effects on the basic Doniach
picture [10]: in addition to the ratio of the bare Kondo scale
T (0)
K to the RKKY interaction scale I [the horizontal axis of
Fig. 1(a)], there is a second axis characterized by a quantity G
that measures the quantum Ô¨Çuctuations of the local-moment
magnetism. The GPD naturally incorporates Kondo destruc-
tion and the associated local quantum criticality [6, 11], which
describes a direct transition (at temperature T = 0) between
(i) a paramagnetic phase with a large Fermi surface (PL) in-
duced by the Kondo effect and (ii) an antiferromagnetically
ordered phase with a small Fermi surface (AFS) produced
by Kondo destruction. Such a phase diagram appears perti-
nent to a variety of strongly correlated systems beyond heavy
fermions, such as the organic charge-transfer salts [13] and
high-Tc cuprates [14].

The GPD has had considerable inÔ¨Çuence on the understand-
ing and classiÔ¨Åcation of quantum critical heavy-fermion met-
als with respect to variations of dimensionality [15], geomet-
rical frustration [16‚Äì20], or otherwise tunable quantum Ô¨Çuctu-
ations [21‚Äì24]. However, there have been very few concrete
theoretical calculations investigating the nature of the phase
transitions. An important open question [5, 8, 9] is whether
there is indeed a line of local critical points where the Kondo
destruction occurs simultaneously with the formation of long-
range order, as crossed by the Type-I trajectories shown in
Fig. 1(a), or only a single point that is multi-critical.

In this work, we present the Ô¨Årst theoretical study of this
outstanding issue, carried out by incorporating into an Ising-
anisotropic Kondo lattice model the effect of tunable quantum
Ô¨Çuctuations induced by a transverse magnetic Ô¨Åeld. Our main

We close with several remarks. Firstly, our results provide
a concrete realization of a line of type-I transitions in the pro-
posed global phase diagram in Fig. 1. They also demonstrate
a superscaling of the magnetic response that implies that the
critical exponents are unchanged along this line.
Secondly, the Kondo lattice model [Eq. (1))] in the limit of
JK ‚Üí 0 exhibits, for sufÔ¨Åciently large ‚àÜ, a spin-gapped para-
magnetic phase [27]. We therefore expect the emergence of a
PS phase for large ‚àÜ and small JK. However, this regime is
difÔ¨Åcult to study, because the ‚àÜ-induced transition at T = 0
in the absence of any coupling to fermions is Ô¨Årst-order in
EDMFT (see the Supplemental Material [39]).
Indeed, for
values of ‚àÜ(0) approaching O(1), the second-order transitions
described above become Ô¨Årst-order. A uniÔ¨Åed treatment of
type-I and type-III transitions on the global phase diagram,
corresponding to weaker and stronger transverse Ô¨Åelds respec-
tively, is an important issue deferred to future studies.

Finally, our concrete calculations using a well-deÔ¨Åned
model are important for laying a theoretical foundation for the
overall understanding and classiÔ¨Åcation of quantum-critical
heavy-fermion metals. By providing evidence for a line of
local critical points on the global phase diagram, our Ô¨Ånd-
ings contribute a robust basis for understanding the unusual
properties characteristic of Kondo-destruction quantum crit-
icality observed in various heavy-fermion compounds. Fur-
thermore, our study suggests a new experiment, namely, to

To summarize, we have identiÔ¨Åed a concrete Kondo lattice
model in which the dynamical effects of tunable quantum Ô¨Çuc-
tuations of the local-moment magnetism can be studied on an
equal footing with those associated with the Kondo coupling.
We have demonstrated, for the Ô¨Årst time in a concrete model,
a line of Ô¨Åxed points describing local quantum critical points,
where the onset of antiferromagnetic order is concurrent with
the destruction of Kondo entanglement. This result provides
a strong basis for the classiÔ¨Åcation of quantum phase transi-
tions of antiferromangetic heavy-fermion metals in terms of
the global phase diagram, and motivates a new experiment to
test our predictions.

IV. MARGINAL BEHAVIOR IN THE SELF-CONSISTENT SOLUTIONS

In the previous section we discussed the emergence of a line of unstable Ô¨Åxed points for the standalone BFKM with Ô¨Ånite
values of the bare transverse Ô¨Åeld. Here, based on the general correspondence between the standalone and self-consistent
versions of the BFKM, we argue that a similar line of Ô¨Åxed points appears in the solutions of the EDMFT equations, i.e., that a
Ô¨Ånite transverse-Ô¨Åeld introduces an additional marginal operator in the lattice case as well.

As shown in Refs. S6 and S7, for the self-consistent BFKM with ‚àÜ = 0, the leading logarithmic scaling form of the local
critical susceptibility is determined by the self-consistency condition [Eq. (9)]. Up to irrelevant terms, the correspondence
between the leading low-frequency form of the spectral function B(œâ) [Eq. (S1)] and the Weiss Ô¨Åeld œá‚àí1
0 (œâ) implies that
self-consistent critical solutions form a subset of the standalone model.

On general grounds, we expect that self-consistent critical solutions are also a subset of solutions of standalone models both
for zero and nonzero transverse Ô¨Åelds. The results presented in the main text indicate that for certain critical combinations
of the couplings JK, I, and ‚àÜ, the system exhibits the dynamical logarithmic form of the zero transverse-Ô¨Åeld solution. Two
possibilities then arise: either the set of self-consistent critical solutions is mapped onto a single effective ‚àÜ = 0 unstable Ô¨Åxed
point or, for each value of the transverse-Ô¨Åeld, the RG Ô¨Çows are determined by different Ô¨Åxed points. In either case, the leading
critical scaling must be compatible with that of the ‚àÜ = 0 solution, with an expected renormalization of nonuniversal scales.
Equivalently, ‚àÜ is either irrelevant about the zero-transverse Ô¨Åeld unstable Ô¨Åxed point or it introduces a marginal scaling variable.
In view of the results of the standalone BFKM with ‚àÜ > 0, we believe that the latter scenario is more likely. The superscaling
shown in Figs. 4(a) and 4(b) is consistent with the presence of a marginal operator.

In practice, the distinction between irrelevant and marginal cases is difÔ¨Åcult to establish unambiguously due to the heavy
computational demands in the vicinity of the critical point. The difference between the two possibilities is important from a
physical point of view. In the irrelevant case, the Ô¨Çuctuations of the local moments, parameterized by ‚àÜ, cannot tune a relevant
scaling variable that allows any transverse Ô¨Åeld (however large) to modify the critical low-energy theory. In the marginal case,
as discussed in the previous section, in the progressive splitting of the spectrum with increasing |‚àÜ| suggests a gradual disentan-
glement of the critical modes associated with Kondo destruction, and an evolution to a Ô¨Åxed point increasingly dominated by the
Ô¨Çuctuations of the local moment. A priori, this makes possible the AFS ‚Üî PS, PL ‚Üî PS transitions that are expected for large
transverse Ô¨Åelds.

For completeness, we also discuss the possibility that ‚àÜ introduces an additional relevant operator. Close to the self-consistent
‚àÜ = 0 Ô¨Åxed point, the modiÔ¨Åed RG Ô¨Çows become unstable in a new direction, eventually heading to a different Ô¨Åxed point. If
this point is stable, the second-order transition must become a cross-over. Alternately, a second unstable Ô¨Åxed point would most
likely generate a new dynamical critical form. The fact that the local susceptibility appears to maintain its ‚àÜ = 0 logarithmic
form, the static superscaling shown in Fig. 4(b), and the natural expectation of marginal behavior discussed above, all make this
scenario unlikely.

V. DEPENDENCE OF CRITICAL EXPONENT Œ± ON NRG DISCRETIZATION PARAMETER Œõ

Both in its original form [S1] and its Bose-Fermi (two-chain) extension [S2], the NRG involves a discretization parameter
Œõ > 1. Its essential role is to provide an artiÔ¨Åcial separation of energy scales along the Wilson chain representation of the
conduction band and of the bosonic bath. The values of certain properties calculated using the NRG results depend on Œõ, and
the physical values must be obtained via extrapolation to the continuum limit Œõ ‚Üí 1. The exponent Œ± entering Eq. (10) is one
such property. An NRG treatment of a pure-bosonic mapping of the self-consistent BFKM in zero transverse Ô¨Åeld discovered a
logarithmic dependence of Œ± on Œõ [S13]. The good Ô¨Åt in that case (based on multiple Œõ values spanning half a decade) motivated
the analogous procedure used in the present work, where the extrapolation to Œõ = 1 was based solely on data points for Œõ = 3
and Œõ = 9.
