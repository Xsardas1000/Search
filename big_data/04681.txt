THE DERIVED CATEGORY OF COMPLEX PERIODIC K-THEORY

LOCALIZED AT AN ODD PRIME

IRAKLI PATCHKORIA

Abstract. We prove that for an odd prime p, the derived category D(KU(p)) of the p-local
complex periodic K-theory spectrum KU(p) is triangulated equivalent to the derived category
of its homotopy ring œÄ‚àóKU(p). This implies that if p is an odd prime, the triangulated category
D(KU(p)) is algebraic.

1. Introduction

The category of modules Mod-KU over the complex periodic K-theory spectrum KU has been
studied for at least thirty years. Using the fact that the homotopy ring œÄ‚àóKU is isomorphic to
the Laurent polynomial ring Z[u, u‚àí1], |u| = 2, and the universal coeÔ¨Écient theorem, one can give
a complete algebraic description of the homotopy category Ho(Mod-KU ) = D(KU ), also known
as the derived category of KU . This description essentially goes back to BousÔ¨Åeld [3, 4]. Wolbert
in [40] elaborated BousÔ¨Åeld‚Äôs ideas in terms of modern notions of ring and module spectra. The
basic idea is that the isomorphism type of any object X ‚àà D(KU ) is completely determined by
the isomorphism type of the homotopy œÄ‚àóX, considered as a graded œÄ‚àóKU -module. Furthermore,
using the universal coeÔ¨Écient theorem, morphisms in D(KU ) admit an algebraic characterization
in terms of matrices consisting of elements of Hom and Ext groups over œÄ‚àóKU . Parallel to this,
one can also consider the category of diÔ¨Äerential graded modules over œÄ‚àóKU ‚àº= Z[u, u‚àí1]. The
homotopy category of the latter, called the derived category of œÄ‚àóKU and denoted by D(œÄ‚àóKU ),
has very similar formal properties to those of D(KU ). The isomorphism class of an arbitrary
object M ‚àà D(œÄ‚àóKU ) is determined by the isomorphism type of the homology H‚àóM , considered
as a graded œÄ‚àóKU -module. Moreover, the morphisms in D(œÄ‚àóKU ) can also be described in terms
of matrices consisting of elements of certain Hom and Ext groups over œÄ‚àóKU .

This analogy between D(KU ) and D(œÄ‚àóKU ) turns out to be a shadow of a much stronger

statement. It follows from [16, 4.3.2] and [40] that there is an equivalence of categories

D(KU ) ‚àº D(œÄ‚àóKU ).

In fact, the results of [40] and [16] yield many other equivalences of similar type. Let R be an A‚àû-
ring spectrum. Suppose that the graded global homological dimension of the homotopy ring œÄ‚àóR
is at most one and assume that œÄ‚àóR is concentrated in even degrees. Then there is an equivalence
of categories

D(R) ‚àº D(œÄ‚àóR),

where D(R) is the derived category of R (the homotopy category of module spectra over R) and
D(œÄ‚àóR) is the derived category of œÄ‚àóR (the homotopy category of diÔ¨Äerential graded modules
over œÄ‚àóR). Examples of such A‚àû-ring spectra include periodic complex topological K-theory
KU , periodic Adams summands E(1) (also known as the Johnson-Wilson spectra of height one),
connective Morava K-theories k(n) and p-local real periodic topological K-theory KO(p) for an
odd prime p. We note that if the inÔ¨Ånite loop space ‚Ñ¶‚àûR is not a product of Eilenberg-Mac
Lane spaces, then the model category Mod-R is not Quillen equivalent to the model category of
diÔ¨Äerential graded modules over œÄ‚àóR. Hence the equivalence D(R) ‚àº D(œÄ‚àóR) does not come from
a zig-zag of Quillen equivalences if R = KU, E(1), k(n), or KO(p).

It follows from the result of Schlichting [32] (see also [26]) that the associated model

We would also like to remind the reader that in contrast to Theorem 1 and Theorem 2, there
are examples of model categories which have the rigidity property that their homotopy categories
admit a unique model up to Quillen equivalence. Examples include the model categories of spec-
tra [33], K(2)-local spectra [30], and modules over Eilenberg-Mac Lane ring spectra (see [36]).
Moreover, the paper [34] shows that the homotopy category of spectra, also known as the stable
homotopy category, is not algebraic.

Finally, before describing the methods of the proofs, we brieÔ¨Çy mention connections of Theorem
1 with C‚àó-algebras. It follows from [5] and [9] that the thick subcategory B generated by the
C‚àó-algebra C inside the KK-category (also known as the bootstrap class) embeds into D(KU )
fully-faithfully. The papers [2] and [23] pose the question of whether the triangulated category B
is algebraic. Theorem 1 implies that if we p-localize the category B at an odd prime p, then the
resulting triangulated category B(p) is algebraic.

Methods of the proofs. The proof of Theorem 2 is based on the ideas of Franke [13] and
the results of the paper [27]. We make essential use of the theory of Grothendieck derivators,
which seems to be the most convenient homotopy-theoretic language for this paper. This is not
surprising since none of the equivalences above come from Quillen equivalences of model categories
or equivalences of (‚àû, 1)-categories.

The general Theorem 4.2.3 in Section 4 which implies Theorem 2 needs the assumption that the
homotopy ring œÄ‚àóR is concentrated in degrees divisible by some number N ‚â• 4. Hence Theorem
4.2.3 does not apply to KU or KU(p), since œÄmKU is nontrivial for every even m. The strategy
for proving Theorem 1 is to reduce to the case of KO(p). More precisely, we use that

Since the homotopy groups of KO(2) and KO are much more involved than those of KO(p) for
an odd p, our methods do not apply in the 2-local or integral case. More precisely, KO(2) and KO
have homotopy groups in odd degrees and the global homological dimension of the homotopy rings
is inÔ¨Ånite. We do not know whether the 2-local or integral version of Theorem 1 holds. This still
remains an open problem. We also do not know whether proving the 2-local version of Theorem 1
implies the integral statement since the fracture squares are non-canonical in general triangulated
categories. However, we believe that the method which will solve the problem for KU(2) will most
likely be applicable already integrally.

4.3. Examples. In this subsection we list some examples to which Theorem 4.2.3 applies.

Example 4.3.1. Let p be an odd prime. Consider the p-local real periodic K-theory spectrum
KO(p). The spectrum KO(p) admits an A‚àû-structure (even an E‚àû-structure). The homotopy
ring œÄ‚àóKO(p) is isomorphic to the graded Laurent polynomial ring Z(p)[v, v‚àí1], where the element
v has degree 4. Hence the graded global homological dimension of œÄ‚àóKO(p) is equal to 1 and
it is concentrated in degrees divisible by 4. This implies that the model category Mod-KO(p)
together with the compact generator KO(p) ‚àà D(KO(p)) satisÔ¨Åes the conditions of Theorem 4.2.3
(see Example 2.2.2). We conclude that the functor

R : D(Z(p)[v, v‚àí1]) ‚Üí D(KO(p))

realizes a triangulated equivalence. Finally, we note that the model categories Mod-KO(p) and
Mod-Z(p)[v, v‚àí1] are not Quillen equivalent, since the inÔ¨Ånite loop space BO(p) of KO(p) is not
a product of Eilenberg-MacLance spaces (see e.g., [27, Corollary A.1.3]).

Example 4.3.2. Let p be a prime and n a positive integer. Assume that either p is odd or
n 6= 1. Consider the connective Morava K-theory spectrum k(n) at the prime p. According to
[22], the spectrum k(n) admits an A‚àû-structure. The homotopy ring œÄ‚àók(n) is isomorphic to the
graded polynomial algebra Fp[vn], where the degree of vn is equal to 2(pn ‚àí 1). This implies that
Mod-k(n) and the compact generator k(n) ‚àà D(k(n)) satisfy the conditions of Theorem 4.2.3.
Hence we conclude that if either p is odd or n 6= 1, then the functor

R : D(Fp[vn]) ‚Üí D(k(n))

is a triangulated equivalence. If p = 2 and n = 1, then the functor R is an equivalence of categories
([27, Example 5.3.2]), but it still remains open whether R is triangulated.

We again note that this triangulated equivalence does not come from a Quillen equivalence (see

e.g., Example 5.3.2 of [27]).
