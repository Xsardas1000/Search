Abstract

We consider two families of planar self-similar tilings of diï¬€erent nature: the tilings
consisting of translated copies of the fractal sets deï¬ned by an iterated function system, and
the tilings obtained as a geometrical realization of a topological substitution (an object of
purely combinatorial nature). We establish a link between the two families in a speciï¬c case,
by deï¬ning an explicit topological substitution and by proving that it generates the same
tilings as those associated with the Tribonacci Rauzy fractal.

1 Introduction
1.1 Main result and motivation
Self-similar tilings of the plane are characterized by the existence of a common subdivision rule for
each tile, such that the tiling obtained by subdivising each tile is the same as the original one, up
to a contraction. These tilings have been introduced by Thurston [Thu89] and they are studied
in several ï¬elds including dynamical systems and theoretical physics, see [BG13]. A particular
class of self-similar tilings arises from substitutions, which are â€œinï¬‚ation rulesâ€ describing how to
replace a geometrical shape by a union of other geometrical shapes (within a ï¬nite set of basic
shapes). Among these, an important class consists of the planar tilings by the so-called Rauzy
fractals associated with some one-dimensional substitutions. These fractals are used to provide
geometrical interpretations of substitution dynamical systems. They also provide an interesting
class of aperiodic self-similar tilings of the plane, see [PF02, BR10].

The aim of this article is to establish a formal link between two self-similar tilings constructed

from two diï¬€erent approaches:

â€¢ Using an iterated function system (IFS), that is, specifying the shapes and the positions
of the tiles with planar set equations (using contracting linear maps), which deï¬ne the
tiles as unions of smaller copies of other tiles. In particular, an IFS does make use of the
Euclidean metric of the plane.
â€¢ Using a topological substitution, that is, specifying which tiles are allowed to be neighbors,
and how the neighboring relations are transferred when we â€œinï¬‚ateâ€ the tiles by substitution
to construct the tiling. With this kind of substitution, there is no use in anyway of a the
Euclidean metric: the tiles do not have a metric shape (they are just topological discs).

In other words, we tackle the following question:

Given a tiling deï¬ned by an IFS, is there a topological substitution which generates an
equivalent tiling? If yes, how can we construct it? In other words, when is it possible
to describe the geometry of a self-similar tiling (geometrical constraints) by using a
purely combinatorial rule (combinatorial constraints) ?

1.2 Comparison of some diï¬€erent notions of substitutions
The word â€œsubstitutionâ€ is used in many diï¬€erent ways in the litterature. The list below contains
several such notions, going from the most geometrical one (IFS) to the most combinatorial
one (topological substitutions). This list is not complete, it only contains the notions of
substitutions that we explicitly use in this article. See [Fra08] for another survey about geometrical
substitutions.

One-dimensional symbolic substitutions These substitutions are used to generated inï¬nite
one-dimensional words which are studied mostly for their word-theoretical and dynamical
properties. An example is the Tribonacci substitution 1 7â†’ 12, 2 7â†’ 13, 3 7â†’ 1 deï¬ned in
Section 3.3. See [PF02] for a classical reference. This is the only notion of the present list which
is only symbolic (not geometrical).

Self-aï¬ƒne substitutions (iterated function systems) Also known as substitution Delone
sets [LW03], this notion is a particular class of iterated functions systems, where it is required
that the geometrical objects deï¬ned by the IFS are compact sets which are the closure of their
interior, in such a way that tilings can be deï¬ned. See Proposition 3.5 for an example of such a
deï¬nition for the Tribonacci fractal.

Dual (or â€œgeneralizedâ€) substitutions These substitutions, introduced in [AI01] can be
seen as a discrete version of self-aï¬ƒne substitutions. Instead of deï¬ning fractal tilings in a purely
geometrical way (like with IFS), these substitutions act on unions of faces of unit cubes located
at integer coordinates. We deï¬ne the associated fractal sets and tilings by iterating the dual
substitution and by taking a Hausdorï¬€ limit of the (renormalized) unions of unit cube faces.
The fact that we deal with unit cube faces allows us to exploit some ï¬ne combinatorial and
topological properties of the resulting patterns. This provides some powerful tools in the study
of substitution dynamics and Rauzy fractal topology. Dual substitutions are usually denoted by
Eâˆ—

and results, and Deï¬nition 3.2 for the particular example studied in this article.

Local substitution rules This notion has been used to tackle combinatorial questions about
substion dynamics [IO93, ABI02, ABS04, BBJS15] and have also been studied in a more general
context [Fer07, JK14]. Their aim is to get a â€œmore combinatorialâ€ version of dual substitutions.
Instead of computing explicitely the coordinates of the image of each unit cube face (like we do
for dual substitutions), we give some local rules (or concatenation rules) for â€œgluing togetherâ€
the images of two adjacent faces. The map deï¬ned in Figure 10, p. 19 is an example of such a
substitution (except that it is deï¬ned over topological tiles and not unit cube faces).

Topological substitutions
Introduced in [BH13], topological substitutions do not make any
use of geometry: the tiles are topological disks (with no Euclidean shape), the boundaries of
which have a simplicial structure (made of vertices and edges). It is a notion less geometrically
rigid than the previous ones. They act on CW-complexes, and the â€œgluing rulesâ€ are more
abstract and combinatorial than local substitution rules. A topological substitution generates a
CW-complex homeomorphic to the plane. If this complex can be geometrized as a tiling of the
plane, we say that the tiling is a topological substitutive tiling. Topological substitutions allowed
for instance to prove that there is no substitutive primitive tiling of the hyperbolic plane, even
though an explicit example of a non-primitive topological substitution which generates a tiling of
the hyperbolic plane is given in [BH13].

The examples of topological substitutions given in the present article (Figure 4 and Figure 12)
are interesting because they provide new examples of topological substitutive tilings, which can
be realized as (substitutive) tilings of the plane.

1.3 Organization of the article
From the point of view of the previous section, we establish a link between the IFS Rauzy fractal
tiling and the tiling deï¬ned by topological substitutions ï¬rst by going from IFS substitution to
local substitution rules (using dual substitutions) and then making a link between these local
rules and the topological substitution.

In Section 3 we deï¬ne the Tribonacci substitution and its associated dual and IFS geometrical
substitutions. In Section 4 we deï¬ne topological substitutions and the Tribonacci topological
substitution we are interested in. The link between the IFS and the topological substitution is

other Rauzy fractal tilings, and we explain that ï¬nding a suitable topological substitution has
some dynamical implications for the underlying one-dimensional substitution.

Links with dynamics of substitutions
It is interesting to remark that being able to ï¬nd
such a topological substitution has some implications on the dynamics of the underlying Pisot
symbolic substitution. Indeed, it can be proved that the underlying Pisot substitution has pure
discrete spectrum if and only if the patterns generated by its associated dual substitution contain
arbitrarily large balls [BR10]. This property is diï¬ƒcult to check for dual substitutions, but easy
to check for topological substitutions (see the core property in Section 4).

This is linked to an important topic in substitution dynamics. There are many equivalent
statements of the pure discrete spectrum property and they attract a lot of interest, notably in
the context of the Pisot conjecture [ABB+15].
