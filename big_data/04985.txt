Abstract. For any ï¬eld K and for a completely arbitrary graph E, we characterize
the Leavitt path algebras LK(E) that are indecomposable (as a direct sum of two-sided
ideals) in terms of the underlying graph. When the algebra decomposes, it actually does
so as a direct sum of Leavitt path algebras for some suitable graphs. Under certain
ï¬niteness conditions, a unique indecomposable decomposition exists.

1. Introduction

Leavitt path algebras can be regarded as the algebraic counterparts of the graph C âˆ—-
algebras, the descendants of the algebras investigated by J. Cuntz in [17], which have
had much attention from analysts in the last two decades (see [25] for an overview of
the subject). Leavitt path algebras can also be viewed as a broad generalization of the
algebras constructed by W. G. Leavitt in [23] to produce rings without the Invariant Basis
Number property (i.e., whose modules have bases with diï¬€erent cardinality).

The Leavitt path algebra LK(E) was introduced in 2004 in the papers [1] and [9]. LK(E)
was ï¬rst deï¬ned for a row-ï¬nite graph E (countable graph such that every vertex emits
only a ï¬nite number of edges) and a ï¬eld K. Despite a relatively recent introduction,
they have already generated quite a bit of activity. The main directions of research
include: characterization of algebraic properties of a Leavitt path algebra LK(E) in terms
of graph-theoretic properties of E; study of the modules over LK(E); computation of
various substructures (such as the Jacobson radical, the center, the socle and the singular
ideal); investigation of the relationships with C âˆ—(E) and general C âˆ—-algebras; classiï¬cation
programs; study of the K-theory; and generalization of the constructions and results ï¬rst
from row-ï¬nite to countable graphs and ï¬nally, from countable to completely arbitrary
graphs. For examples of each of these directions see for instance [14] and the references
therein.

In the current paper we focus on the ï¬rst and last of these lines: concretely, we give a
graph-theoretic characterization of the ring-theoretic property of being indecomposable,
and we do that in the most general context of arbitrary graphs.

Many times in the literature, the graph-theoretic conditions characterizing some ana-
lytic property of the graph C âˆ—-algebra (for instance, simplicity in [16] or purely inï¬nite
simplicity in [21]) turned out to be the same graph-theoretic condition characterizing the
corresponding algebraic version of these properties (simplicity in [1] and purely inï¬nite
simplicity in [2]). Even though some eï¬€orts are currently being made to obtain some kind
